Sensor Effectiveness Across Terrains

The system's strength lies in deploying the right sensors for the specific environment. This chart compares the operational effectiveness of each sensor type across the most common challenging topographies.

Sensor & Communication Matrix

This matrix provides specific recommendations for sensor and communication deployment based on environmental and geographical conditions.

Condition Sensor Configuration Communication Method
Mountains
  • Thermal/Visual Cameras: Long-range surveillance from elevated positions.
  • Seismic Sensors: Detect ground vibrations in valleys and trails.
  • Laser Communication: Primary link for high-bandwidth backhaul across clear valleys.
  • RF Links: Redundant links and short-range communication in obstructed areas.
Dense Forests
  • Acoustic & Seismic Sensors: Primary detection under dense canopy.
  • Thermal Cameras: Augment detection by capturing heat signatures through foliage.
  • RF Communication: Primary mode for resilient, obstacle-penetrating mesh networks.
  • Optical Fiber: Secure, fixed links for major hubs if possible.
Deserts / Open Plains
  • Long-Range Thermal/Visual: Extensive range for wide-area surveillance.
  • Radar: Long-range vehicle and object detection, less affected by sand storms.
  • Laser Communication: Highly effective and reliable primary backhaul due to clear line-of-sight.
  • RF Links: Redundancy to handle temporary dust storm disruptions.
Fog, Rain & Dust
  • Radar & Acoustic: Reliable for detection in low-visibility weather.
  • Thermal/IR Cameras: Reduced effectiveness, but still capture heat signatures.
  • RF Communication: The most reliable option, as its signals penetrate atmospheric particles.
  • Optical Fiber: Unaffected by weather, used for secure, fixed connections.
